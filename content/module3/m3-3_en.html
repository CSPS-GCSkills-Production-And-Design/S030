Common Terminology


1. Common Terminology

Common Terminology helps to define:

  • Organizational functions represent the first area where common terminology is important. Major functions and functional units with incident management responsibilities are named and defined, and terminology for the organizational elements involved is standard and consistent

  • Resource descriptions, such as personnel, facilities, and equipment/supply items, are given common names and are “typed” or categorized by their capabilities. This helps to avoid confusion and enhance interoperability

  • Incident facilities

  • Position titles, ICS management or supervisory positions are referred to by common, standardized titles, such as Officer, Chief, Director, Supervisor, or Leader.

 

Use plain language or clear text

Do not use radio codes, agency-specific codes or jargon

​​​​​​​Even if you use radio codes on a daily basis, why should you use plain language during an incident response?

  • More than one agency involved

  • Major obstacles in communications

  • Variety of codes and acronyms

  • Not every code is the same

  • Not every acronym has the same meaning

  • Confusion results


Use of Position Titles

  • Common standard for performance expectations. For example, if one agency uses the title Branch Chief, another Branch manager etc., this lack of consistency can cause confusion at the incident.

  • Helps to ensure that qualified individuals fill positions rather than by seniority.

  • Standardizes communication/position titles are useful when requesting qualified personnel. For example, in deploying personnel, it is important to know if the positions needed are Unit leaders, Division Supervisors, etc.

  • Describes the responsibilities of the position, such as Communications Unit Leader, it makes it easy to determine the basic responsibilities of the position.
