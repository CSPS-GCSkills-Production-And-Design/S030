4. Unified Command

The command function may be carried out as a:

Single Command

The first possibility is a single command in which the Incident Commander will have complete responsibility for incident management. A single Command may be very simple involving only an Incident Commander and a couple of single resources, or it may be a complex organizational structure with an Incident Management Team.

Single Command

  • Agency 1

   

Unified Command

The second possibility is a Unified Command in which responding agencies and/or jurisdictions with responsibility for the incident share incident management.

Unified Command

  • Agency 1
  • Agency 2
  • Agency 3


Unified Command

  • Enables a common set of incident objectives and strategies

  • Allows joint decision making by establishing a single command structure

  • Maintains unity of command, each employee only reports to one supervisor

Unified Command may be needed for incidents involving:

  • Multiple jurisdictions

  • A single jurisdiction with multiple agencies sharing responsibility

  • Multiple jurisdictions with multi-agency involvement 

If a Unified Command is needed, Incident Commanders representing agencies or jurisdictions that share responsibility for the incident manage the response from a single Incident Command Post. Under a Unified Command, a single coordinated Incident Action Plan will direct all activities. The Incident Commanders will supervise a single Command and General Staff organization and speak with one voice.

A Unified Command allows agencies with different legal, geographic, and functional authorities and responsibilities to work together effectively without affecting individual agency authority, responsibility, or accountability.

In summary, Unified Command is an important element in multijurisdictional or multiagency incident management:

  • Provides guidelines to enable effective with different legal, geographic, and functional responsibilities to coordinate, plan, and interact effectively.

  • Overcomes inefficiency and duplication of effort that can occur when agencies from different functional and geographic jurisdictions, or agencies at different levels of government, operate without a common system or organizational framework.

  • Enables participation and contribution to the process of determining overall incident strategies; selecting objectives; and ensuring that joint tactical planning occurs.

  • Does not compromise or neglect legal authorities.

Example of unified command​​​​​​​​​​​​​​

This example of Unified Command shows the organization chart of the Moyie Complex interface fire near Cranbrook, British Columbia during the firestorms of 2003.

Legend : 

BCFS stands for the British Columbia Forest Service, and OFC stands for the Office of the Fire Commissioner. The Office of the Fire Commissioner was represented by the local Fire Department in Cranbrook, so where this acronym exists it is actually a local Fire Department.


You will note that the Unified Commanders came from the two primary agencies fighting fires. The Operations Section Chief is from BC Forest Service because the primary problem was several large forest fires that threatened homes.

To ensure clear communications and to coordinate firefighting activities, a Deputy Operations Section Chief was assigned from the Office of the Fire Commissioner. This carried on the concept of Unified Command.

The RCMP, in charge of the Evacuation Branch, reported to the Operations Section Chief, who was from BC Forest Service. This was to ensure clear communications as to where the fire was spreading. The RCMP could also have been part of Unified Command.
