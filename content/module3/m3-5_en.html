Chain of Command and Unity of Command


3. Chain of Command and Unity of Command

Chain of Command

  • Clear line of authority within the ranks of the incident management organization, with lower levels subordinate to, and connected to, higher levels. On many incidents, the organizational structure for operations will consist of only Command and Single Resources.

  • Eliminates confusion or contradictory orders being given by clearly identifying who individuals report to and who is responsible to deliver orders.

  • Communication lines from authority to immediate subordinates and likewise back up from the subordinates to their immediate supervisor.

Chain of command supports the principle of unity of command.

As incidents expand, the chain of command is established through an organizational structure. This structure can consist of several layers as needed, such as Command, Sections, Branches, Divisions or Groups, Units and Single Resources.



 

Every individual has a designated supervisor to whom they report at the scene of the incident.

Personnel:

  • Report to only one supervisor.

  • Receive work assignments only from their supervisor.

Together, the principles of chain of command and unity of command help to clarify reporting relationships and eliminate the confusion caused by multiple, conflicting directives. Incident managers at all levels must be able to control the actions of all personnel under their supervision. Although orders and other formal communications must flow through the chain of command, members of the organization may directly communicate with each other to ask for or share information through informal communications.
