Integrated Communications

 

11. Integrated Communications

  • Ensure responders can communicate with one another during an incident.

  • Ensure interoperability between agencies, communication equipment, procedures, and systems must operate across jurisdictions.

Developing an integrated voice and data communications system, including equipment, systems and protocols, must occur prior to an incident.

As previously discussed, every incident requires an Incident Action Plan, and the Communications Plan is an important part of this IAP. Like the rest of the IAP, the Communication Plan can be very simple and stated verbally, or it can be quite complex, and form a part of a written IAP.

Example:

Example

During the interface fires in British Columbia in 2003, there were problems with the compatibility of various systems preventing complete communications between responders from various regions of the province and from out of province. Specific problems encountered with communication equipment included the following:

  • Some of the responding fire departments used proprietary systems that did not function in remote areas.
  • Some designated VHF frequencies could not be programmed into some of the newer radios.
  • Many fire departments had interoperable radio systems but police, ambulance, forestry and air support at times lacked this same compatibility.

One of the recommendations from the review team was that the province of BC achieve emergency radio interoperability across agencies throughout the Province.

Effective ICS communications include:

  • Modes: The “hardware” systems that transfer information.

  • Planning: Planning for the use of all available communications resources.

  • Networks: The procedures and processes for transferring information internally and externally.

Types of Communications Resources:

There are several types of communications resources that are available during an incident, including radio systems and frequencies, telephone systems, computers, and message runners, coding and signaling. Please click on each type for more detailed information.

Radio Systems and Frequencies

Radio Systems and Frequencies include stationary and mobile radios, dispatch consoles, handheld radios, pagers, repeater systems, satellites, dishes, infrared systems, low and high band frequencies, and all the supporting equipment needed to maintain a system.

These systems have some disadvantages. Even the most technologically advanced radio system is likely to suffer from poor coverage areas, loss of signal strength in certain kinds of building construction, and overloading. Information transmitted via radio is not secure: many media outlets and private citizens monitor police and fire frequencies.

Telephone Systems

Telephone Systems include digital and analog hardwired systems, wireless cellular and satellite systems, and telephone line-dependent adjuncts such as computers, fax machines, and mobile devices.

Telephone systems have their limitations as well.
Emergency response organizations must create and maintain accurate phone lists. In a recent US national exercise, incident communications were delayed by out-of-date emergency telephone directories. Landline availability is often limited and only a few emergency lines may be available at any one time. Phone systems can often be overloaded during an emergency as public use is increased and systems may have to shut down. In the case of cellular phones, although newer technology exists, public wireless networks are not designed to handle emergency traffic volumes. Some tower systems run on battery backup systems and will only stay operational for 8-12 hours after a power outage.

Computers

Computers and tablets obviously have enormous value with regard to communications but they also have their limitations.

These limitations include incompatible systems and software, dependence on power and phone lines, administrative restrictions, security and access issues, dependence on wireless connectivity for data, and lack of familiarity with certain systems on the part of human operators.

Message Runners, Coding and Signaling

Message Runners, Coding, and Signaling are often forgotten forms of communications, these remain viable options for when other systems fail.

However, the amount of information that can be transmitted is very limited, and transmission using these methods is not as fast as when using other systems. Coding and signaling require trained personnel, and there are few people who have the necessary training.


Radio Interoperability  -->  Communications Networks

Several communication networks may be established depending on the size and complexity of the incident. These may include Command Net, Tactical Net, Support Net, Ground-to-Air, and Air-to-Air. Please click on each for more information.

Command Net

Command Net is established to link supervisory personnel from the Incident Commander down to, and including, division and group supervisors.

Tactical Nets

Tactical Nets are established in a variety of ways, such as, by agency, department, geographical area, or function. Tactical nets may be established for each branch, or for divisions and groups, depending upon hardware and frequency availability, and specific incident needs.

Support Nets

Support Nets are established on larger incidents to handle logistics traffic and resource status changes.

Ground-to-Air

Ground-to-Air is established to coordinate ground-to-air traffic.

Air-to-Air

Air-to-Air is assigned for coordination between aircraft assigned to an incident.
