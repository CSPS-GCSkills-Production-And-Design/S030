Operations Section: Modular Structure


The Operations Section is unique in that it expands from the bottom up unlike the rest of the ICS organization.

Initially, the Operations function usually consists of a few resources first assigned to an incident (initially reporting to the Incident Commander). As additional resources are committed and the incident becomes more complex, the Operations Section may be established.

Expanding from the bottom up, the Operations Section will add needed levels of supervision as more resources are assigned. Resources can be organized in three ways: Single Resources, Strike Teams and Task Forces.

At some point, the Operations Section and the rest of the ICS organization will grow smaller. The decision to reduce the size of the organization will be based on the achievement of tactical objectives. Demobilization planning begins with the activation of the first personnel and continues until the ICS organization ceases operation. Resources can be released from the incident once their tactical objectives have been met and the organization contracts as easily as it expanded.

Single Resource

  • Individuals

  • A piece of equipment and its personnel complement

  • A crew or team of individuals with an identified supervisor

Strike Team

  • Resources of the same kind and type

  • Use common communications

  • Led by a Strike Team Leader

The foreknowledge that all elements have the same capability and the knowledge of how many will be applied allows for better planning, ordering, utilization and management.

Strike Teams are part of the Operations Section and follow the Span of Control guidelines.

Note : 

“Kind” of resource refers to a general description of the resource. For example: Patrol vehicle, helicopter, fire engine, bull dozer etc.

“Type” of resource refers to its performance capability.

Resources are typed by a number with 1 having the highest capability or capacity, 2 the next highest, and so on.

Task Force

  • Combination of mixed resources (different Kinds and Types)

  • Use common communications

  • Led by Task Force Leader

  • versatile combinations of resources

As with Strike Teams, Task forces are part of the Operations Section and follow the Span of Control Guidelines. Combining resources into Task Forces allows for several resource elements to be managed under one individual’s supervision, thus reducing the span of control for the Supervisor.

Divisions, Groups & Branches

Divisions

Divisions are used to divide an incident into geographical areas of operation.

  • Divided geographically, such as the area around a stadium, the floors of a building or an open area.

  • Usually labeled using alphabet characters (A, B, etc.). Other identifiers may be used, such as east, west, north, and south, as long as they are known by assigned responders.

  • Managed by a Supervisor

Note : The important thing to remember about ICS Divisions is that they are established to divide an incident into geographical areas of operation.

Groups

Groups are used to describe functional areas of operation.

  • Established based on the needs of an incident

  • Labeled according to the functional job they are assigned

  • Not limited geographically

  • Resources are managed as Single Resources, Strike Teams or Task Forces

  • Managed by a Supervisor

Example : Some examples of functional groups could include: medical groups, search and rescue groups, perimeter security groups, and maritime salvage groups.

Divisions & Groups

A method of organizing resources within the Operations Section is the combined use of geographic divisions and functional groups. Divisions and Groups can both be used together on an incident.

This approach is commonly used when a functional activity operates across divisional lines. For example, a Shoreline Cleanup Group could be used wherever required and moved as needed through more than one Division along the shore.

  • Can be used together on an incident

  • At equal level in the organization

Note : Divisions and Groups are at an equal level within the organization. One does not supervise the other. When a Group is working within a Division on a special assignment, Division and Group Supervisors must closely coordinate their activities.

Branches

Branches are used when the number of divisions or groups exceeds the span of control and can be either geographical or functional.

  • Established if Divisions / Groups exceed span of control

  • Have functional or geographic responsibility

  • Identified by Roman numerals or functional name

  • Managed by Branch Directors. Branch Directors may have deputy positions as required. In multi-agency incidents the use of Deputy Branch Directors from assisting agencies can be of great benefit to ensure and enhance interagency coordination.

While span of control is a common reason to establish Branches, additional considerations may also indicate the need to use Branches.

Multidisciplinary incidents

  • And finally, very large incidents may be organized using geographic or functional Branches in order to maintain span of control.

Multijurisdictional incidents

  • Secondly, there are Multijurisdictional incidents where it may be better to organize the incident around jurisdictional lines. In these situations, Branches may be set up to reflect jurisdictional boundaries.

Very large incidents

  • In the case of Multidisciplinary incidents where multiple disciplines such as Firefighting, Medical, and Public Works are involved, this may create the need to set up incident operations around a functional Branch structure.
